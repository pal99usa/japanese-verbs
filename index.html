Japanese Verb Groups

All Japanese verbs fall into one of three groups. The group determines how you conjugate — once you know the pattern, it applies to every verb in that group.

Group 1
U-verbs / Godan — the largest group
Dictionary ends in: ~u
Masu stem: drop ~u, add ~imasu

iku, nomu, yomu, kaku, hanasu

⚠ Some -eru/-iru verbs are G1 — no shortcut!

Group 2
Ru-verbs / Ichidan
Dictionary ends in: ~eru / ~iru
Masu: drop ~ru, add ~masu

taberu, miru, neru, wasureru, oriru

Group 3
Irregular — only 2 roots
kuru → kimasu (to come)
suru → shimasu (to do)

Suru compounds: kekkon suru, benkyou suru...

Te-form Rules — Group 1

Group 2 is easy: drop -ru, add -te. Group 1 uses 5 sound patterns based on the masu-stem ending.

1. Stem ends in i / chi / ri → tte
~imasu / ~chimasu / ~rimasu → ~tte
aimasu → atte tachimasu → tatte tsukurimasu → tsukutte kaerimasu → kaette
2. Stem ends in ni / mi / bi → nde
~nimasu / ~mimasu / ~bimasu → ~nde
shinimasu → shinde nomimasu → nonde asobimasu → asonde
3. Stem ends in ki → ite  Exception: iku → itte!
~kimasu → ~ite
kikimasu → kiite kakimasu → kaite arukimasu → aruite ikimasu → itte ⚠
4. Stem ends in gi → ide
~gimasu → ~ide
isogimasu → isoide oyogimasu → oyoide
5. Stem ends in shi → shite
~shimasu → ~shite
hanashimasu → hanashite keshimasu → keshite
Analogy: sound smoothing The te-form picks the easiest ending to pronounce after each stem sound. The n/m/b → nde rule exists because all three are nasal sounds — your nose resonates on all of them — so the te-form stays nasal. Your mouth is already in position.

Ta-form follows identical rules

Same sound rules as the te-form. Just swap the final -e for -a.

Stem endingTe-formTa-formExample
i / chi / rittettamatte → matta
ni / mi / bindendanonde → nonda
kiiteitakaite → kaita
giideidaisoide → isoida
shishiteshitahanashite → hanashita

Essential New Forms

Five high-frequency forms and patterns you will encounter constantly in everyday Japanese.

Tai-form — "Want to V"

Very Common
nani wo tabetai? — what do you want to eat? nihon ni ikitai — I want to go to Japan netai — I want to sleep
GroupVerbTai-formMeaningHow to form
G2taberutabetaiwant to eatdrop -ru + tai
G2mirumitaiwant to watchdrop -ru + tai
G1nomunomitaiwant to drinkmasu stem + tai
G1ikuikitaiwant to gomasu stem + tai
G1kaukaitaiwant to buymasu stem + tai

G3kurukitaiwant to comeki + tai
Key rule — same stem as masu-form: For G2: tabemasu → tabe + tai. For G1: nomimasu → nomi + tai. Just swap -masu for -tai.
Tai-form conjugates like an i-adjective: ikitai → ikitakunai (don't want to go) → ikitakatta (wanted to go) → ikitakunakatta (didn't want to go)

Example sentences

sushi wo tabetai desu.
すしをたべたいです。
I want to eat sushi.
nani wo shitai desu ka?
なにをしたいですか？
What do you want to do?
kyou wa doko mo ikitakunai.
きょうはどこもいきたくない。
I don't want to go anywhere today. (casual)
motto hanashitakatta desu.
もっとはなしたかったです。
I wanted to talk more.

Very Common

Group 1 potential — clean rule: The final -u shifts to -eru. Think of it as sliding one row down the vowel chart: u → e + ru.
nomu → nomeru  |  kaku → kakeru  |  matsu → materu  |  oyogu → oyogeru
Two accepted forms for Group 2 potential: Both taberareru (full) and tabereru (ra-drop) are used. The short form is common in casual speech but frowned on in formal writing. You will hear both.

Example sentences

nihongo ga hanasemasu ka?
にほんごがはなせますか？
Can you speak Japanese?
ashita koraremasen.
あしたこられません。
I can't come tomorrow.
kono kanji ga yomemasu ka?
このかんじがよめますか？
Can you read this kanji?
piano ga hikeru you ni naritai.
I want to become able to play piano.

~te imasu — Two meanings, one structure

Essential
Common confusion point: ~te imasu has two distinct meanings that English speakers often mix up. Context usually clarifies which one applies — but you need to know both exist.
MeaningExampleHiraganaEnglish
1. Ongoing action
(happening right now)		tabete imasuたべていますI am eating (right now)
hashitte imasuはしっていますI am running (right now)
nani wo shite imasu ka?なにをしていますか？What are you doing?
2. Resultant state
(result of completed action)		mado ga aite imasuまどがあいていますThe window is open (= was opened)
kekkon shite imasuけっこんしていますI am married (got married and am in that state)
shitte imasuしっていますI know (came to know; now in knowing state)
Analogy: Think of ~te imasu like English "is ___-ing/ed": "is eating" (ongoing) vs "is broken" (result). Same structure, two meanings. shitte imasu doesn't mean "I am knowing" in a weird sense — the act of learning already happened and you're now in the state of knowing.
Critical: shirimasu vs shitte imasu Never say shirimasu to mean "I know." That implies future tense — "I will come to know." Always use: shitte imasu (I know) or shirimasen (I don't know).

Which group is this verb? — Decision tree

Practical Skill

Use this flowchart when you encounter an unfamiliar verb. Start at Step 1.

Step 1
Is the verb kuru or suru — or a compound ending in ~suru?
e.g. benkyou suru, kekkon suru, renraku suru
YES →
Group 3 — irregular. Memorise kuru and suru; all compounds follow suru.
Step 2
Does the dictionary form end in something other than -eru or -iru?
e.g. nomu, kaku, hanasu, matsu, kau, isogu
YES →
Group 1 — definite. Any verb ending in plain -u (not -eru/-iru) is always Group 1.
Step 3
Ends in -eru or -iru? Most are Group 2 — but some common ones are Group 1 exceptions.
Check list: kaeru (go home), hairu, hashiru, kiru (cut), shiru, shaberu, iru (need)...
On list →
Group 1 exception — memorise these individually. See "Watch Out" tab for the full list.
Not on list →
Group 2 — safe assumption for any -eru/-iru verb you haven't seen before.

Plain Form vs Polite Form

Polite form (-masu) is for most conversations. Plain form is for casual speech AND as the building block for complex grammar. Both are essential.

na-Adjectives and Nouns (e.g. yuumei — famous)

In casual short replies, drop the da. Say yuumei? / un, yuumei. — not yuumei da?

Why "plain" not "casual"? Plain form appears inside complex grammar even in formal speech:
taberu kamo shiremasen — I might eat.
taberu to omoimasu — I think I will eat.
tabeta koto ga arimasu — I have eaten it before (experience).
The plain form lives inside these structures regardless of register.
